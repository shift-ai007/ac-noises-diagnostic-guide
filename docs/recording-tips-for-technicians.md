# How to Record AC Noises for Your Technician

When you call for service, the noise might stop by the time the technician arrives. A short phone recording is worth more than any verbal description. Here's how to capture useful audio.

## Recording Basics

1. **Use your phone's voice recorder or video camera** — video is better because it shows where the sound is coming from.
2. **Get close to the source** — hold your phone 1-2 feet from where the noise is loudest. Background noise (TV, traffic, kids) makes diagnosis harder.
3. **Record at least 30 seconds** — some noises are intermittent. Capture a full cycle if possible.
4. **Record from multiple positions** — the same noise sounds different from the outdoor unit vs. inside near the air handler.

## What to Capture

### Outdoor Unit Recordings

Stand on each side of the outdoor unit (if accessible) and record separately. Label them:

- **Compressor side** — usually where refrigerant lines enter the unit
- **Fan side** — top of the unit where the fan exhausts air
- **Electrical side** — where the wiring and disconnect box are located

### Indoor Recordings

- **At the thermostat** — captures the overall sound level in the living space
- **At the air handler** — usually in a closet, attic, or garage. This is where blower motor and evaporator coil sounds originate
- **At a supply vent** — put your phone right next to a vent to capture ductwork noise

## Describe What You Hear

Even with a recording, text descriptions help. Use these common terms:

| Sound Type | Description | Common Cause |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Banging | Metallic impact, like a hammer | Loose component in compressor |
| Buzzing | Electrical hum, steady | Contactor, capacitor, or wiring |
| Clicking | Relay engaging, rhythmic | Normal startup or failing contactor |
| Grinding | Metal on metal, harsh | Failed bearings, debris in fan |
| Gurgling | Liquid bubbling, like a drain | Refrigerant issue or drain line |
| Hissing | Gas escaping under pressure | Refrigerant leak |
| Rattling | Loose hardware vibrating | Loose screws, panels, or debris |
| Screeching | High-pitched squeal | Worn belt or failing motor bearings |
| Whistling | Air through a narrow gap | Duct leak or filter issue |

## Timing Information to Note

Your technician will ask these questions. Have the answers ready:

- **When did it start?** — Today, last week, gradually over months?
- **Is it constant or intermittent?** — Does it happen every cycle or randomly?
- **Does it change with temperature?** — Worse on hotter days? Only below a certain temp?
- **Does it happen at startup, during running, or at shutdown?**
- **Have you changed anything recently?** — New filter, thermostat adjustment, furniture blocking vents?

## Sending Recordings

Most HVAC companies accept recordings via:

- Text message to their service number
- Email attachment
- Uploaded through their website contact form

Send the recording before your appointment. A technician who's heard the sound in advance can bring the right parts and tools on the first visit, saving you a return trip.
